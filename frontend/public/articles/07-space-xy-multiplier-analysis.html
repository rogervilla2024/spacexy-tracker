The X/Y Axis System

Space XY's signature feature is its dual-axis coordinate system. Let's break down what this means and how it functions.

What the Axes Represent

  • Y-Axis (Vertical): Represents the rocket's altitude or vertical position

As the rocket launches and travels through space, its position is tracked on both axes simultaneously. The game displays these coordinates alongside or combined into the multiplier value. The rocket's path creates a trajectory through the two-dimensional coordinate space.

The X and Y coordinates work together to produce the multiplier value displayed to players. As both values increase, the multiplier grows. The exact relationship between the coordinates and the final multiplier is determined by BGaming's algorithm.

How the Multiplier Works

Understanding how the multiplier functions helps demystify the game.

Starting Point

Every round begins at 1.00x (or equivalent X/Y coordinates representing this value). This is the minimum multiplier - if you cash out immediately, you simply get your bet back with no profit.

Multiplier Growth

Once the round starts, the multiplier begins climbing. The rate of increase isn't constant - it may accelerate or have varying speeds depending on the visual implementation. However, the crash point is predetermined before the round begins by the random number generator.

The Crash Point

Every round has a predetermined crash point determined by the RNG. This value is hidden until the crash occurs. The crash point can be:

  • As low as 1.00x (instant crash)
  • As high as 10,000x (maximum multiplier)
  • Anything in between, following a probability distribution

Payout Calculation

If you cash out before the crash, your payout is:

Payout = Bet Amount x Cash-Out Multiplier

For example: $10 bet x 5.00x multiplier = $50 payout ($40 profit)

Multiplier Distribution

The distribution of crash points follows a mathematical pattern designed to produce the 97% RTP.

Theoretical Distribution

Crash games use an inverse probability distribution. Lower multipliers have higher probability; higher multipliers have lower probability. This is necessary to maintain the house edge.

Key Observations

  • Half crash below 2x: Approximately 50% of all rounds crash at or below 2.00x
  • 80% below 5x: Only 20% of rounds reach above 5.00x
  • 90% below 10x: Just 10% of rounds reach above 10.00x
  • 99% below 100x: Reaching triple-digit multipliers is very rare

Why This Distribution?

This distribution is mathematically necessary to maintain the 97% RTP. If high multipliers were more common, the RTP would exceed 100% and the game would lose money. The precise calibration ensures the casino retains its 3% edge.

Common Questions About Multipliers

Can I predict multipliers based on the X/Y display?

No. The X/Y display is a visualization of the predetermined crash point, not a separate system that can be analyzed. The crash point is determined by RNG before the round starts.

Are higher multipliers "due" after many low crashes?

No. This is the gambler's fallacy. Each round is independent. The probability of any crash point is the same every round, regardless of history.

Should I target low or high multipliers?

Mathematically, it doesn't matter - all targets have the same expected value. Choose based on your preference for variance: low targets give frequent small wins, high targets give rare large wins.

Why cap at 10,000x?

Maximum multipliers exist to limit operator liability. While 10,000x is lower than some competitors, the probability of reaching extreme multipliers is so low that the practical impact is minimal.

Does the X/Y system affect the crash point?

No. The crash point is determined by the RNG. The X/Y system is purely visual - it displays the multiplier in a different way but doesn't influence what that multiplier will be.

Can I find patterns in multiplier history?

Any patterns you see are coincidental. The RNG produces random results that will naturally include apparent patterns, but these have no predictive value. Past multipliers provide zero information about future outcomes.
